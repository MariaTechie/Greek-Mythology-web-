Primordial Gods

The primordial gods, or Protogenoi, in Greek mythology were the fundamental elements that formed the universe at creation. These beings were often elemental in nature, embodying concepts like earth, sky, sea, time, and more. They were sometimes depicted in anthropomorphic forms but were inseparable from their respective elements. Ananke represented necessity and inevitability, and with Chronos (time), they encircled creation. Chaos, the Protogenos of the lower air, gave birth to other elements like Erebus (darkness) and Nyx (night). Eros, the god of procreation, sparked the universe's creation. Gaea, the Earth, was the first being to emerge, and Hemera, goddess of the day, scattered the dark mists of night to reveal the world. Nyx, goddess of night, cloaked the heavens with darkness, while Erebus was the mist of darkness within the earth. Oceanus, the great river, encircled the earth, and Ourea represented the mountains. Pontus was the god of the sea, and Tethys was associated with the flow of fresh water. Other primordial gods include Phanes, the creator-god of generation, and Uranus, the solid dome of the sky, whose reign was later overtaken by his son Cronus.
